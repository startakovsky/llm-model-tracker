Same model. 30 prices.
Price buys you nothing.

Thirty providers serve the exact same GLM-5.2 weights on OpenRouter. Their prices span 3.2×. Their reliability spans 86% to 100%. The two have almost nothing to do with each other.

2026-07-20 · all figures from one live OpenRouter API call

A screenshot of a single provider set this off: Decart, serving GLM-5.2, showing an availability figure in the low 80s while everyone assumes an API endpoint is just "up." So we pulled the live per-provider data for GLM-5.2 straight from OpenRouter and looked at all of it at once.

The result is more interesting than one bad provider. It is that the price you pay for an open-weight frontier model is not telling you what you think it's telling you.

The cheapest provider (DeepInfra, $0.93) runs a healthy 99%. The least reliable (Decart, 86.4% — the lowest of all 30) sits in the expensive half at $1.20. Even first-party Z.AI charges $1.40, near the top of the range. Paying more is not buying you uptime, and paying less is not costing you uptime.

About that one provider

Decart's trailing-24h availability read 86.4% — the single lowest of the 30, and roughly 13 points below the pack that clusters at 98–100%. That figure is confirmed twice: the OpenRouter screenshot showed ~85%, and the live API independently returned 86.4%. So the degradation is real and it is an outlier.

What we deliberately are not saying: that Decart was "down for a day." An availability percentage is not a duration, and OpenRouter does not publish what the number's denominator is (failed requests vs. minutes unreachable). Eighty-six percent could be many brief hiccups or one longer window — we can't tell, so we won't claim. The defensible statement is narrow and sufficient: Decart is the reliability outlier of the group, by a clear margin, despite being priced above the median.

⚠ Assumptions & limits — stated up front

  1. What "86.4%" means is undefined. It's OpenRouter's trailing-24h availability metric; the exact denominator isn't public. We treat it only as a peer-relative comparison, never as downtime duration.
  2. We don't know why Decart degraded. Capacity, a bad deploy, a hardware fault — all plausible. We report the number, not a cause.
  3. Prices and uptime are a single-moment snapshot (2026-07-20). Both move; this is not a long-run average.
  4. Historical price swings (see footnote) come from this tracker's own logging and are not independently corroborated here.

Why this happens — and what OpenRouter actually does

OpenRouter presents GLM-5.2 as one endpoint and, by default, uses price-based load balancing: per its own docs, requests are "load balanced across the top providers to maximize uptime," weighted toward cheaper ones. It is not winner-take-all to the single cheapest provider, and it is not a reliability guarantee. It spreads your traffic — which means some of it can still land on the 86% provider unless you say otherwise.

That is the practical trap. The single-endpoint abstraction hides a marketplace of 30 operators of wildly varying quality behind one clean model name and one headline price. The headline price is roughly the cheapest offer, not the one you'll necessarily be served, and it carries no information about whether that provider stays up.

A model's list price on OpenRouter is a floor set by its cheapest operator — not a spec of the service you'll actually receive.

The opinion worth sharing

Stop reading a single price off the model page and assuming it describes the service. Behind that number sit dozens of operators whose reliability ranges from 86% to 100% and does not track price at all. If uptime matters to your workload, pin your providers explicitly and treat the default load balancer as a convenience, not a guarantee. On a commoditized open-weight model, choosing the operator is the real decision. Price is the distraction.
